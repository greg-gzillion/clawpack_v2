# Humphrey's Executor v. United States, 295 U.S. 602 (1935)

## Case Summary
**Citation**: 295 U.S. 602 (1935)
**Court**: Supreme Court of the United States
**Date**: May 27, 1935
**Vote**: 9-0 (Sutherland writing)

## Issue
Whether the President may remove a Federal Trade Commissioner before the expiration of his statutory term for reasons other than those specified in the FTC Act.

## Facts
- William Humphrey appointed FTC Commissioner for 7-year term
- FTC Act provided removal only for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office"
- President Roosevelt removed Humphrey for policy disagreements
- Humphrey died, executor sued for back pay

## Holding
**No. Congress may limit the President's removal power for officers of independent regulatory agencies who exercise quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions.**

## Key Quote
> "The FTC is an administrative body created by Congress to carry into effect legislative policies... Such a body cannot in any proper sense be characterized as an arm or an eye of the executive. Its duties are performed without executive leave and, in the contemplation of the statute, must be free from executive control."

## Distinction from Myers v. United States (1926)
| Myers | Humphrey's Executor |
|-------|-------------------|
| Postmaster (purely executive) | FTC Commissioner (quasi-legislative/judicial) |
| President has unlimited removal power | Congress may limit removal |
| Executive branch officer | Independent agency officer |

## Significance
- **Foundation of Independent Agencies**: Established constitutional basis for agencies independent of presidential control
- **Removal Protection**: FTC, SEC, FCC, NLRB commissioners protected from at-will removal
- **Survives Today**: Still good law, cited in Seila Law (2020)

## Related Cases
- **Wiener v. United States (1958)**: Extended to War Claims Commission
- **Morrison v. Olson (1988)**: Independent counsel removal limits upheld
- **Free Enterprise Fund v. PCAOB (2010)**: Dual for-cause removal unconstitutional
- **Seila Law v. CFPB (2020)**: Distinguished - single-director agency different

## Practical Implications
- Multi-member commissions have removal protection
- Single-director independent agencies do not (Seila Law)
- Agency design affects presidential control
