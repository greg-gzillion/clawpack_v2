# Northern Pipeline Construction Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U.S. 50 (1982)

## Case Summary
**Citation**: 458 U.S. 50 (1982)
**Court**: Supreme Court of the United States
**Date**: June 28, 1982
**Vote**: 6-3 (Brennan writing for plurality)

## Issue
Whether the Bankruptcy Act of 1978's broad grant of jurisdiction to bankruptcy courts violated Article III of the Constitution.

## Facts
- Northern Pipeline filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy
- Brought state law contract claim against Marathon Pipe Line in bankruptcy court
- Marathon challenged bankruptcy court's constitutional authority to adjudicate state law claims
- Bankruptcy court and district court upheld jurisdiction

## Holding
**Yes. The 1978 Act's broad grant of jurisdiction to bankruptcy judges violated Article III.** Bankruptcy judges (Article I) cannot exercise the judicial power of the United States over state law claims.

## Key Quote
> "The judicial power of the United States must be vested in Article III courts... Congress cannot vest jurisdiction over Northern Pipeline's state law contract claim in a non-Article III court."

## Plurality Reasoning (Brennan)
1. **Article III Requirements**: Judicial power must be exercised by judges with life tenure and salary protection
2. **Public Rights Exception**: Does not apply to state law contract claims between private parties
3. **Adjunct Theory**: Bankruptcy courts not mere "adjuncts" to district courts

## Concurrence (Rehnquist/O'Connor)
- Narrower holding: Congress cannot vest Article III power in Article I courts for this type of claim
- Did not reach broader constitutional questions

## Aftermath
- **Emergency Rule**: Judicial Conference adopted interim rule (valid for 2 years)
- **BAFJA of 1984**: Congress enacted current system (28 U.S.C. ｧ 157)
  - Bankruptcy courts as "units" of district courts
  - Core/non-core distinction
  - District court reviews de novo

## Relationship to Stern v. Marshall (2011)
| Northern Pipeline (1982) | Stern v. Marshall (2011) |
|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| Broad constitutional challenge | Narrower holding |
| 1978 Act struck down | Core/non-core refined |
| Led to 1984 Amendments | Confirmed constitutional limits remain |

## Significance
- **Foundational Case**: Established constitutional limits on bankruptcy court power
- **Public vs. Private Rights**: Framework for analyzing Article III questions
- **Still Cited**: Central to understanding bankruptcy court jurisdiction
