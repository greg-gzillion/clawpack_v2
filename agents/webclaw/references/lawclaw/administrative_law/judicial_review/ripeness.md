# Ripeness Doctrine - Administrative Law

## Overview
Ripeness determines when agency action is ready for judicial review. Prevents premature judicial intervention.

## Two-Part Test - Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (1967)
1. **Fitness for Judicial Review**: Are issues purely legal? Is action final?
2. **Hardship to Parties**: Would withholding review cause immediate harm?

## Key Cases
- **Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (1967)** - Established modern ripeness test
- **Toilet Goods Ass'n v. Gardner (1967)** - Companion case, no pre-enforcement review
- **Gardner v. Toilet Goods Ass'n (1967)** - FDA color additive regulations
- **Reno v. Catholic Social Services (1993)** - Immigration regulations not ripe
- **Ohio Forestry Ass'n v. Sierra Club (1998)** - Forest plan not ripe
- **National Park Hospitality Ass'n v. Dept. of Interior (2003)** - Purely legal issue ripe
- **Texas v. United States (2023)** - DACA case, ripeness considerations

## Pre-Enforcement Review
- Generally available for final agency action
- Purely legal questions more likely ripe
- Fact-intensive challenges less likely ripe
- **Key Case**: Abbott Laboratories (1967)

## Exhaustion vs. Ripeness
| Doctrine | Focus | Timing |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Ripeness | When review is appropriate | Before agency final action? |
| Exhaustion | Whether remedies used | Did party complete process? |

## Fitness Factors
- Is action final agency action?
- Are issues purely legal?
- Would further factual development help?
- Is agency still formulating policy?

## Hardship Factors
- Direct and immediate impact on day-to-day business
- Choice between compliance and penalties
- Irreparable harm if review withheld
- Cost of compliance alone not sufficient hardship

## Exceptions and Special Cases
- **Facial Challenges**: More likely ripe
- **As-Applied Challenges**: Usually require enforcement
- **Constitutional Claims**: May ripen earlier
- **Interim Rules**: Often ripe upon issuance
