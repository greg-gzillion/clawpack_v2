# Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U.S. 837 (1984)

## Case Summary
**Citation**: 467 U.S. 837 (1984)
**Court**: Supreme Court of the United States
**Date**: June 25, 1984
**Vote**: 6-0 (Marshall, Rehnquist, O'Connor not participating)

## Issue
Whether courts must defer to an agency's reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statute it administers.

## Facts
- Clean Air Act required states to regulate "stationary sources" of pollution
- EPA defined "stationary source" as entire plant (bubble concept)
- NRDC challenged, arguing each smokestack was separate source
- D.C. Circuit set aside EPA's interpretation

## Holding
**Yes. Courts must defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes.**

## The Chevron Two-Step Framework
1. **Step One**: Has Congress directly spoken to the precise question?
   - If yes ? Court and agency must follow congressional intent
   - If ambiguous ? Proceed to Step Two

2. **Step Two**: Is the agency's interpretation reasonable?
   - If yes ? Court defers to agency
   - If no ? Court may substitute its own interpretation

## Key Quote
> "If Congress has explicitly left a gap for the agency to fill, there is an express delegation of authority to the agency to elucidate a specific provision of the statute by regulation. Such legislative regulations are given controlling weight unless they are arbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute."

## Significance
- Became the most cited administrative law decision in history
- Foundation of judicial deference to agency interpretations for 40 years
- Applied in over 18,000 judicial decisions
- **Overturned by Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024)**

## Chevron's Demise
- **Loper Bright v. Raimondo (2024)**: Chevron overturned
- Courts now exercise independent judgment on statutory interpretation
- Agency interpretations may still be persuasive (Skidmore deference)
