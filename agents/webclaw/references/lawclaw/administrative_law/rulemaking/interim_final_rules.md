# Interim Final Rules - Emergency Rulemaking

## Overview
Interim final rules (IFRs) allow agencies to issue rules without prior notice-and-comment when immediate action is necessary. They become effective upon publication while accepting post-promulgation comments.

## Official Resources
- **APA § 553(b)(B)** - Good cause exception
- **ACUS IFR Report**: https://www.acus.gov/report/interim-final-rules

## Legal Standard - "Good Cause"
Agency must find notice-and-comment is:
- **Impracticable**: Emergency situation (public health, safety)
- **Unnecessary**: Minor, non-substantive changes
- **Contrary to Public Interest**: Delay would cause harm

## IFR Requirements
- **Immediate Effective Date**: Rule effective upon publication
- **Post-Promulgation Comment Period**: Usually 30-60 days
- **Statement of Good Cause**: Agency must explain why IFR needed
- **Commitment to Revise**: Agency commits to consider comments and modify rule

## Judicial Review of IFRs
- Courts review good cause determination de novo
- Skeptical scrutiny of agency claims of emergency
- Must be "genuine emergency" not agency-created urgency

## Key Cases
- **American Federation of Gov't Employees v. Block (D.C. Cir. 1981)**: Good cause requires genuine emergency
- **Council of Southern Mountains v. Donovan (D.C. Cir. 1981)**: Emergency must be external to agency
- **Sorenson Communications v. FCC (D.C. Cir. 2014)**: Agency cannot create own urgency

## Examples of Valid IFRs
- COVID-19 public health rules (CDC, OSHA)
- National security emergencies
- Statutory deadlines requiring immediate implementation
- Correction of drafting errors

## Post-IFR Process
1. Rule effective immediately upon publication
2. Agency accepts comments for 30-60 days
3. Agency reviews comments and publishes final rule
4. Modifications made in response to comments
5. Final rule replaces IFR

## Risks of IFRs
- **Judicial Rejection**: Courts may vacate for inadequate good cause
- **Procedural Challenges**: Failure to provide meaningful comment opportunity
- **Remedies**: Court may vacate IFR and require notice-and-comment
