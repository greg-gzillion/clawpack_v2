# Regulatory Flexibility Act (RFA) - 5 U.S.C. ｧｧ 601-612

## Overview
The Regulatory Flexibility Act requires agencies to analyze the impact of proposed rules on small businesses, small governmental jurisdictions, and small organizations.

## Key Requirements

### Initial Regulatory Flexibility Analysis (IRFA) - ｧ 603
Must be published with NPRM and include:
1. Reason for agency action
2. Objectives and legal basis
3. Estimate of small entities affected
4. Compliance requirements (reporting, recordkeeping)
5. Relevant federal rules that duplicate/overlap/conflict
6. Significant alternatives considered

### Final Regulatory Flexibility Analysis (FRFA) - ｧ 604
Must be published with final rule and include:
1. Need for and objectives of rule
2. Significant issues raised by public comments
3. Response to comments from Chief Counsel for Advocacy
4. Estimate of small entities affected
5. Description of steps taken to minimize economic impact
6. Alternatives considered and reasons for rejection

### Certification - ｧ 605
Agency may certify rule will not have "significant economic impact on a substantial number of small entities"
- Must provide factual basis for certification
- Chief Counsel for Advocacy may challenge certification

| Small Business | SBA size standards (NAICS-based) | 13 CFR ｧ 121.201 |

## Judicial Review - ｧ 611
- Agency compliance with RFA is **judicially reviewable**
- Small entities may petition for judicial review within 1 year
- Courts may defer agency action to allow compliance
- Remand for RFA violations common

## Key Cases
- **Small Business Regulatory Enforcement Fairness Act (SBREFA) of 1996**: Added judicial review
- **AFL-CIO v. OSHA (D.C. Cir. 1990)**: RFA applies to OSHA standards
- **Colorado v. EPA (10th Cir. 2023)**: RFA analysis must be adequate
- **Nat'l Women's Law Ctr. v. OMB (D.D.C. 2019)**: RFA reporting requirements

## Periodic Review - ｧ 610
Agencies must review rules every 10 years that have significant economic impact on small entities.
